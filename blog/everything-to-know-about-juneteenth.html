Everything to Know about Juneteenth

Published June 2025

Juneteenth, also known as Freedom Day or Emancipation Day, is a significant event in American history that commemorates the end of slavery in the United States. This holiday is celebrated on June 19th each year and marks the day in 1865 when Union soldiers arrived in Galveston, Texas, to announce the end of the Civil War and enforce the emancipation of enslaved African Americans.

Everything to Know about Juneteenth

Historical Background

The origins of Juneteenth trace back to the issuance of the Emancipation Proclamation on January 1, 1863, which declared that all enslaved people in the Confederate states were to be set free. However, it wasn't until June 19, 1865, that the news reached Texas. Major General Gordon Granger arrived in Galveston to take command of the area and enforce the newly adopted policies, delivering General Order Number 3, which proclaimed freedom for all enslaved individuals.

Significance of Juneteenth

Juneteenth holds immense cultural and historical significance as it symbolizes the long struggle for African Americans' freedom and equality. It serves as a reminder of the resilience and perseverance of those who fought against oppression. While it marks the official end of slavery, the ongoing fight for equality, justice, and civil rights continues to resonate with people today.

Celebrations and Traditions

Juneteenth celebrations often include various activities such as family gatherings, barbecues, parades, and educational events. Traditional foods, music, and dance are also a crucial part of the festivities, reflecting African American culture. Some common practices include:

  • Reading of the Emancipation Proclamation
  • Prayer and reflection
  • Community service and volunteer work
  • Storytelling and sharing of personal histories

Juneteenth Becomes a Federal Holiday

In June 2021, Juneteenth was officially recognized as a federal holiday in the United States, making it one of the newest holidays to be added to the federal calendar. This recognition signifies the importance of acknowledging the struggles and contributions of African Americans throughout history, yet it also raises questions about how the holiday will be celebrated and understood in a commercial landscape.

Controversial Opinion: The Commercialization of Juneteenth

While the recognition of Juneteenth as a federal holiday is undoubtedly a step forward, some critics argue that its commercialization undermines the very essence of what Juneteenth represents. Retailers and companies have begun to capitalize on the holiday, with sales and marketing campaigns that risk diluting its historical significance. This commercialization raises concerns about whether Juneteenth will merely become another day of consumerism rather than a time of reflection, education, and meaningful commemoration.

Conclusion

Juneteenth is a vital part of American history that deserves recognition and respect. As we celebrate this holiday, it is essential to remember the sacrifices made for freedom and to continue striving for equality and justice for all. By honoring the significance of Juneteenth, we can help ensure that its legacy endures for future generations.
